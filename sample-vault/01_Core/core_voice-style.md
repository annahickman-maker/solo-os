# Voice and Style Guide

---

## How I Open Videos

**The dominant pattern:** Open with a concrete result or number, then ground it in personal experience. Formula: concrete outcome - credibility anchor - the gap/tension - what the video will fix. No warm-up, no greeting, no hook question.

A secondary pattern is opening with a personal admission of struggle before the result - naming a belief she held about herself that turned out to be wrong.

**Verbatim examples (use these to calibrate):**

- "I have made a grand total of $349,316 selling digital products. But that first $1,000 was by far the hardest to make. It took me almost 2 years to hit that first $1,000. But once I did, within one month, I had made $10,000."
- "In my first year on YouTube, I made a total of $320,080, which is pretty insane, especially considering that I definitely did not get enough views on any of my videos to make any real money from AdSense."
- "So, in April of 2023, I made a little over $6,000 selling my web design services. Fast forward a year from then, in April of 2024, I made a total of $31,643 selling digital products."
- "I am so shy and introverted in my everyday life that when people find out that I do this, I talk to a camera for my job. They are completely shocked."
- "There is a very simple way that I have been able to force myself to be consistent and do hard things. And it's not about having more motivation or more discipline."
- "The only consistent thing about me is that I am consistently beating myself up for not being consistent. And that's a pretty shitty feeling when everyone online is constantly preaching at you to just post every day."

**Variations:**
- Planning/system videos sometimes open with a scene-setting: "All right. So, in this video, I wanted to share with you the planning system I use..."
- Reflective/personal videos open with emotional honesty before numbers: "So 2024 was a huge year of growth for me... And to be honest, it felt a little bit more like an unraveling for me."
- Challenge to common belief: "If you have the goal of taking your online business to 10K months and beyond, this might be the most important video that you watch this year."

**What she never does:** Opens with a greeting ("Hey guys, welcome back"), asks a hollow hook question ("Have you ever wondered why...?"), or thanks viewers for being there.

## Sentence Structure and Rhythm

**The base unit:** Short to medium sentences. She thinks in bursts. A point lands in one or two sentences, then she moves. She does not build long winding arguments - she states a thing, restates it slightly differently, then gives an example.

**Fragments are used deliberately** to land a point hard before moving on:
- "Traffic is the answer to all of your problems."
- "Simple as that."
- "It is consistency over the next three, five or 10 years that is going to get you the results that you want."

**"And so" is the most distinctive structural connector.** It links almost every paragraph of thought. It functions as "therefore" or "which is why" but spoken. Appears 20-50 times per long transcript.

**Repetition for emphasis is a genuine pattern.** She restates the core idea of a point immediately after making it, slightly differently:
- "It just needs to be really, really useful. And a PDF that ChatGPT has made is not useful."
- "The value is in simplicity. And so the easier something is to understand and the simpler it is to use and implement and actually get a really quick win from this thing, the more valuable it becomes."

**"Because" mid-sentence** - she frequently starts a new sentence with "Because" as an explanation bridge:
- "Because the thing is when you are building an audience and you're building a brand..."
- "Because in 2025, if you want to build a business... posting content is how you do that."

**Parenthetical asides** to acknowledge a complication or counter-objection:
- "And I know this is not going to be what you want to hear, especially if you are a multi-passionate creative like me..."
- "Now, this is the mindset woo woo part, but bear with me..."

**Pacing in instructional sections:** She announces the number of steps upfront, walks through each one with a reason before the instruction, and recaps briefly before moving on.

## Sentence Rhythm Pattern

This is the repeating unit her scripts are built from. Every major point follows this sequence:

**1. The landing sentence** - short, direct, often a fragment or a single declarative claim. This is the point stated plainly.
> "Traffic is the answer to all of your problems."
> "The value is in simplicity."
> "Personality beats strategy. Every time."

**2. The "And so" bridge** - one sentence that explains why the landing sentence is true or why it matters. Medium length.
> "And so if no one is seeing what you've built, none of the other stuff matters."

**3. The personal example** - 2-4 sentences. Almost always starts with her own experience, a specific client story with a name, or a before/after scenario with a real date or number. This is the longest part of the unit.
> "For example, in April 2023 I was making $6,000 a month from services. By August, I had my first $20,000 month - and the only thing that changed was that more people were seeing my content."

**4. The restatement** - 1-2 sentences that rotate the angle slightly. Not a repetition - it deepens or reframes what was just said. Often starts with "And so" or "Because."
> "Because when you have the traffic, everything else becomes a testing problem, not a strategy problem."

**5. The opening into the next point** - one sentence that either previews what comes next or names what's still missing. Creates momentum without a hard transition.
> "But knowing you need traffic is not the same as knowing how to get it. So let me show you exactly what I did."

**The rhythm in practice:** short → medium → medium-long → medium → short

Anna does not write five long sentences in a row. She does not write five short sentences in a row. The variation in length is what creates the spoken rhythm. When writing scripts, consciously alternate sentence length within each unit.

**Important:** The restatement (step 4) should not be identical to the landing sentence. It should add a layer - either the consequence, the nuance, or the "so what." A true restatement is not padding; it's the idea landing from a different angle.

## Words and Phrases I Use Often

### Connectors and transitions

**"And so"** - by far the most frequent. Opens new sentences, paragraphs, and thoughts. Functions as a logical bridge.
> "And so, rule number one is stop taking yourself so seriously." / "And so, this was the thing that taught me..."

**"And so then"** - used in step-by-step instructional sections to chain actions.
> "And so then once you've done this, once you've found 30 people to give it to for free..."

**"The thing is"** - introduces a complication, nuance, or reframe.
> "The thing is that while most people know that YouTube is the most powerful platform..." / "But the thing is, this is extremely hard to implement when you're just making a bunch of random videos..."

**"Now"** - signals a pivot to a new point or section. Very common as a paragraph opener.
> "Now, I have never been diagnosed with ADHD..." / "Now, this is the mindset woo woo part..." / "Now, I get a ton of comments from all of you..."

**"But"** - pivots from the outcome/result to the complication or harder truth.
> "But that first $1,000 was by far the hardest to make." / "But here's the thing."

**"So"** as a standalone opener - begins a new section or returns from a digression.
> "So, in April of 2023..." / "So to give you an example..." / "So that's the first thing I want you to do..."

### Softeners and qualifiers

**"Kind of"** - very frequent. Softens observations and self-assessments.
> "I kind of got a little bit burnt out on short form content." / "It kind of felt like every area of my life had kind of unraveled a little bit."

**"A little bit"** - constant softener, especially when describing discomfort or something unflattering.
> "I kind of got a little bit burnt out..." / "Lower your expectations a little bit..."

**"Pretty"** as a modifier.
> "I was pretty inconsistent." / "I have a pretty shitty product..." / "a pretty ambitious goal..."

**"Really"** as an intensifier - often doubled.
> "It just needs to be really, really useful." / "This is really, really important."

**"Especially"** - zeros in on a specific case after a general point.
> "Especially if you are a multi-passionate creative like me..." / "Especially right now when everyone is churning out AI content..."

**"To be very honest with you" / "to be honest with you"** - honesty marker before a harder truth or personal admission.
> "And to be very honest with you, the reason that I wanted to make this video was because I am so tired of seeing all of this content..."

**"To be clear" / "I want to be clear"** - used before clarifying a nuance or potential misread.
> "And I want to be clear that you can still do all of these different things that you want to do..."

### Recurring emphasis patterns

**Stating a thing, then restating it from a different angle before giving the example.** This is her primary explanation rhythm.

**Saying the counter-intuitive version of something, then working back to the reason.**
> "The only advantage I had in this moment over every other web designer out there was that I was visible."

**"This is exactly what..."** - used to close a build and anchor the claim.
> "This is exactly what I did with my products." / "This is exactly what I would tell you to do..."

### Signature phrases

**"And so if you want/need [X], [link/video] is down below."** - her consistent CTA connector.

**"You don't need me. You can absolutely do this without me."** - appears in multiple videos as a disarming pre-CTA softener.

**"Post like a YouTuber but with the messaging and back-end systems of a business owner."** - core framework she returns to across multiple videos.

**"Silly little monkey brain"** - affectionate, self-deprecating reference to her own resistance to hard tasks.
> "It's about having a system that makes it so stupidly easy for your silly little monkey brain that you can't not be consistent..."

**"Zone of genius"** - her term for the specific skill intersection someone should double down on.
> "Pick your zone of genius." / "This is my zone of genius. I refuse to do anything else."

**"The valley of Despair"** - the trough between starting something and seeing results.

**"Cozy lifestyle business"** - her tonal framing for a small, intentional, high-margin business.
> "...build a cozy 20K per month lifestyle business where clients and customers come to you..."

**"A 24-hour sales machine"** - her framework phrase for content that converts passively.
> "...you will create this 24-hour sales machine for your business, even when you are not posting every single day..."

**"Diversification is a distraction"** - a recurring lesson stated as a mantra.

**"Shiny object syndrome"** - her consistent phrase for distraction by new ideas.

## Words and Phrases I Never Use

**What is conspicuously absent:**
- "game-changer"
- "crushing it" / "killing it" / "smashing goals"
- "passive income" used unironically - she actively corrects it: "Passive income doesn't really exist"
- "mindset shift" as a buzzword
- "manifesting" or any law-of-attraction language
- "epic" / "insane" as casual filler (she uses "insane" only for actual numbers)
- Any hustle culture endorsement
- "freedom lifestyle" as a sales-page phrase
- "you guys" as an address
- "Hey, welcome back to my channel"
- "don't forget to like and subscribe" as a hard ask
- "guru" except to mock it: "You don't need another business guru giving you another strategy"

**What she uses instead:**
- Instead of "passive income": "income that doesn't mean I had to take on more clients" / "scalable income" / "a system that generates sales"
- Instead of "crushing it": specific numbers and dates
- Instead of "mindset shift": "changed my belief about myself" / "shifted the story I had in my head"
- Instead of "hustle": "intensity" / "a period of hyperfocus"
- Instead of generic empowerment: personal specifics from her own experience

## How I Explain Concepts

**Primary method: Personal experience before framework.** She almost always introduces a concept by telling you what she did, or what happened to her, before explaining the principle behind it. The story is not decoration - it is the proof.
> "For example, 3 or 4 years ago, I decided that I wanted to build the habit of writing more... But when I started, I had this perception that I wasn't a good writer."

**Before/after structure.** The contrast between where she was and where she ended up appears in virtually every video - often with exact dates and dollar amounts.
> "In April of 2023, I was struggling to even make $100. By August, you can see something started to change..."

**Numbered frameworks.** When she has a system, she announces the number upfront and walks through each part. Her numbered lists always include a reason each step matters before the instruction itself.
> "And so in order to crack your brain so you can actually be more consistent, there are three levels that you need to master."

**Analogies are sparse but concrete.** She does not use extended metaphors - brief comparisons that do a specific job:
> "Your content is basically like taking public transit. It's cheap and will eventually get you where you want to go, but it's going to be slow."
> "A launch is spending your marbles."

**The imaginary specific person.** When explaining audience or positioning, she invites the viewer to imagine a scenario with real-feeling specific details.
> "...if you sat there for days and refreshed Stripe obsessively for 3 weeks straight, convinced that it was broken because no one could possibly not want this offer..."

**Client examples with first names and specific outcomes.**
> "Blaze was a web designer and content creator... made $1,500 in sales from his first digital product launch. And Fab is a fashion stylist who also implemented these exact steps..."

**She explains the why before the how.** Each step gets a sentence about why it matters before she tells you what to do. Consistent across every instructional video.

**She addresses the objection before it forms.** She frequently anticipates what the viewer is thinking and says it out loud, then answers it.
> "And I know what you're probably thinking now. What if no one joins my wait list?"
> "For those of you thinking, if I give everything away for free, why would anybody buy my products or services?"

## How I Handle CTAs

**Tone:** Soft invite, never hard sell. CTA is consistently framed as optional, after the viewer has already received full value.

**Placement:** End of video. Mid-video she may direct to another video, but not to a paid offer.

**She pre-dismisses the need for her product before offering it.** This is a consistent structural move: say you don't need her, then say where to find her.
> "You don't need me. You can absolutely do this without me. But if you do want to join a community... the wait list for Independent Creator is down below in the description. But if not, you can absolutely do it alone."

**Video-to-video CTAs are warm redirects, not pitches.**
> "But if you want to see how I structure my business for freedom and novelty while also still focusing all my attention on converting to one core offer, then I've made an entire video on how I do this here that you can watch after this one."

**She frames the offer as a "fast track", never as a necessity.**
> "If you need help actually implementing this, then grab the template down below." / "...your service or product is like the fast track to help them get there."

**Verbatim CTA examples:**
> "If you want to join a community where we have cozy group calls and you have all of the resources and templates and workflows that I've spent the last 5 years building, then the weight list for Independent Creator is down below in the description."
> "And so if you want to join that, then the weight list is down below."
> "And so if you're ready for that in your own business and you want to join me in the next cohort of Independent Creator, then add your name to the weight list in the description."

## How I Open and Close Value Sections

**Opening a new point:**
Three patterns:

1. "Okay, [rule/step/lesson] number [X] is..." - formal numbered transition
   > "Okay, rule number one, and this is definitely the reason that I had so much quick success..."

2. "Now," followed by the section title - used when pivoting to a new phase
   > "Now, this is the mindset woo woo part, but bear with me..." / "Now, the most important step and the main reason that I failed..."

3. "And so [moving into the next thing]..." - casual flow transition for connected points
   > "And so once you have this tool, the last thing you want to do now is..."

**Between sections she briefly recaps what was just covered** - one sentence naming what was just said before pivoting.
> "So that's the first level of consistency, changing your belief about yourself and your identity."
> "So those are the three main phases that you want to be focusing on over the next 3 months."

**Closing a value point:** She lands it with a single declarative sentence that reframes everything that came before, then opens the next section immediately.
> "And so this is when the sales will start to roll in quicker than you can possibly imagine."

**Re-hooking:** She rarely does this explicitly. Momentum is maintained by the "And so" chain. When she does re-hook, she previews what the next section will solve.
> "But of course, you don't just wake up with all of these new amazing habits just because you believe them to be true. You still need systems that actually make doing the hard thing easy."

## Tone Notes

**Overall emotional register:** Calm, direct, considered. Not excitable. She holds a consistent mid-energy - warm but measured. When most animated, it reads as conviction, not performance.

**She talks to one person.** Even in large full-course videos, the address is singular and intimate: "you watching this" / "if you can relate to this" / "for those of you watching this." She never addresses "my audience" or "you guys."

**She is honest about her own failures more readily than she celebrates her wins.** When she mentions money, it is almost always in service of proving something is possible, not to impress.
> "I am really nothing special. If I can do this, you can absolutely do the same."
> "I am not the best web designer and I never have been."

**She uses profanity functionally, not for edge.** Expletives appear when she is genuinely frustrated, emphatic, or self-deprecating. Never placed for shock or relatability. Sparse enough that when they appear they carry weight.
> "And what the hell are you even doing?" / "Getting on camera scared the hell out of me."

**She is not a guru.** She explicitly calls out guru language and dismisses it. She positions herself as having figured out specific things that worked for her - not as having all the answers.
> "You don't need another business guru giving you another strategy or another business model."
> "Only take advice from people living the life that you actually want."
> "Including me and watching this video - if I'm not doing what you want to do, stop watching now."

**She is not a hype machine.** She never promises easy, quick, or guaranteed. When she offers hope, it is grounded in specific timeframes and effort.
> "I'm not telling you that it's going to be quick or easy. If it were easy, everyone would be doing it."
> "It is definitely not passive and it is definitely not easy."

**What she IS:**
- Direct without being blunt
- Warm without being performative
- Confident without being arrogant
- Vulnerable in precise, purposeful doses
- A business owner teaching from experience, not a teacher performing expertise
- Consistent in values across every video - does not shift register depending on topic

**What she is NOT:**
- Not aspirational in an abstract way - never lifestyle porn
- Not self-congratulatory - results are always in service of proof, not status
- Not urgency-driven in a manipulative way
- Not formal - no academic language, no complex vocabulary
- Not a coach who performs empathy - empathy is shown through specifics, not affirmations

## Patterns That Must Never Be Replicated in Scripts

These are natural speech habits that appear in the transcripts and are fine spoken but would sound wrong written out:

1. **"Kind of" as a constant softener.** In speech: natural humility. In scripts: drains precision. "I kind of got a little bit burnt out" written out sounds uncertain where the spoken version reads as candid.

2. **The doubled "And so then" chain** for connecting multiple instructions. In speech the rhythm carries it. In a script it becomes visually repetitive.

3. **"Really, really" as a double intensifier.** Spoken, the repetition lands. Written, it reads as an editing oversight. Use one "really" or replace with a stronger word.

4. **"A little bit" as a softener on everything.** Spoken: self-deprecating. Written across multiple sentences: weakens the register overall.

5. **Mid-sentence restarts.** She will begin a sentence, pivot, and restart mid-thought. This is natural speech. Do not replicate in scripts.

6. **"You know" as a filler connector.** Fine spoken - filler when written.

7. **Verbal hedges before personal admissions.** "Not going to lie to you" / "to be honest" appear so frequently they lose their signalling function if written into every personal section. Use maximum twice per script.

8. **Repetitive paragraph openers.** In loosely scripted sections she opens consecutive paragraphs with "And so" seven or eight times in a row. Written, this creates monotony. Mix the openers.

9. **"Um" and "like" as filler.** Appear in the more conversational, less scripted videos. These are off-the-cuff speaking patterns only.

10. **Over-qualifying reassurances.** "Don't get me wrong" / "I'm not saying that" / "this isn't to say" are fine in moderation but appear as a reflex after every strong claim in unscripted content. Use once or twice per video, not habitually.
