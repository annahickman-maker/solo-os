# Core IP

The five-step method Anna teaches inside Solopreneur Systems, distilled from the Foundation workshop series. This is the canonical version. Every downstream skill, script, video, and sales asset traces back to one of the two foundational beliefs or one of the five steps.

---

## Transformation

**Who:** Experienced creative freelancers - web designers, illustrators, copywriters, videographers, UX designers, any creative running a real service business with some traction and some audience.

**From:** Scattered and overworked. Running the post-content / get-clients / drop-content / start-over treadmill. Watching their follower count grow while their income flatlines. Anxious about AI commoditizing their craft. Buying courses, breaking down what's working for everyone else, telling themselves *"something is wrong with me because I can't be consistent like everyone else."*

**To:** A lean two-hour day. Premium clients paying top dollar for their expertise. A content engine that doubles as a sales engine. Digital products and a community filling in the income gap. A confident expert and thought leader known for one specific thing - not five.

---

## Foundational Beliefs

The two beliefs underneath everything taught here. Every piece of content, every offer, every system traces back to one of these.

### Belief 1 - Personal brand + lean solopreneur business is the best path forward

A personal brand and a lean one-person business is the best path forward for creative freelancers in 2026 - especially in the age of AI. Anyone with skills, knowledge, or expertise needs to have this. It's not optional anymore. AI has commoditized information. The only thing that doesn't commoditize is YOU - your story, your voice, your taste, your reputation around one specific thing. Brand is *"how you make people feel"* and the relationship you build with them at scale. Most people will never buy from you because you have the perfect offer or the perfect landing page. They buy because they know, like, and trust you - and that is the only durable moat left.

### Belief 2 - The Solopreneur OS is the best tool to make this happen

The system, not willpower. The system, not more consistency. The system, not another guru's playbook. The Solopreneur OS is human-first - it works with your brain, your energy, and your life, not against them. AI is in service of both. Built around the operator at the center, so you can sell whatever you put your name to because the system carries you. The business runs while you're offline.

---

## The 5 Steps

The journey Adriana goes on to get from before to after. Five steps, in order: **Positioning -> Offer -> Content -> Launch -> Scaling.** Each one builds on the last. Skip a step and the next one collapses.

---

### 1. Positioning

> *"You set yourself apart by being very specific about who you're talking to and what you help them do. If you try to talk to everyone, you will end up talking to no one. But if you talk to one person really well, you'll end up appealing and relating to a much broader audience."*

**Industry standard:** Position around what you do. *"I'm a web designer."* *"I'm a copywriter."* *"I'm an illustrator."* Try to be everything to everyone. Polish the I-help statement, the niche, the aesthetic. Build the perfect offer, the perfect sales page, the perfect headline - and assume that's what makes people buy.

**Common mistake:** Generic positioning that sounds like every other freelancer in the space. Going so broad in service of *"keeping options open"* that you attract nobody. Or going so niche on a tactic (*"the Fiverr expert"*) that you box yourself in and stop enjoying the work. Worst of all, fixating on the offer, the niche, and the landing page while ignoring the actual thing that makes people pay you: trust. People do not pay $27K because someone has the perfect headline. *"You could take the exact same offer and not get the same results because you do not have the brand that they have built."*

**Anna's POV:** Position around a **transformation**, not a topic, and build a personal brand around it. The transformation is the result your person actually wants - not your service. It has to ladder up to one of the three eternal human desires: health, wealth, or relationships. A Shopify designer's service is *"a Shopify store."* Her transformation is *"more consistent sales so I can quit my day job."*

Underneath that sits the four-layer brand stack Anna teaches in the Lesson 1 diagram:

1. **The person at the center.** Get specific enough that you can picture the exact human sat in front of you - which version of Ben Francis are you talking to, the one hand-sewing in his bedroom in 2012 or the billion-dollar version in 2016? The same niche has very different people inside it and they have very different problems.
2. **The transformation at the top.** What result are you helping them get? This becomes the north star.
3. **What you do + how you help.** Three ingredients to set yourself apart (none of these are must-haves - you can develop them over time): (a) **be specific about what you're best at** - one word people associate with you, (b) **a unique point of view** that pushes back against an industry norm or an enemy you've named, (c) **a unique mechanism** - a named system or method that signals you're not interchangeable.
4. **Trust, built from the bottom up.** Value, results/authority, vulnerability, and connection. *"Your personal brand is just building relationships and networking at scale."*

The strategic version: identify your dream outcome, then work backwards. *"What do I need to be known for to do this? What do I need to do in order to be known? What do I need to learn right now to do that?"* Then start where the leverage actually lives right now - if you only have skills, start with services. Build authority. Then expand. **You don't start with the niche - the niche emerges from working with enough people and doubling down on what worked.**

> **Sub-framework: Three ingredients of differentiation**
> Best-at (one word), point of view (pick an enemy), unique mechanism (name your method). Examples Anna uses: Ali Abdaal = productivity, James Clear = habits, Matt Gray = systems, Matt Gray's "Founder OS" as his named mechanism.

**Why it's better:** It survives AI - your taste, story, and reputation can't be commoditized. It self-selects the right person in and the wrong person out. It lets you go deep with one person, which paradoxically attracts a much broader audience because *"when you serve one person really well, other people start to notice."* It lets you charge premium prices because nobody is interchangeable with you. And it stops you fixating on offers and sales pages when the actual lever is trust.

### 2. Offer

> *"Your offer is more important than your product. How you sell something is more important than what you sell. Nine times out of ten, if something isn't working in your business, it's your offer that needs to change - not your product."*

**Industry standard:** Spend months perfecting the product. Add more deliverables, more templates, more lessons, more modules - because surely *more* equals *more valuable*. Price based on what other people in the niche charge. Or stay stuck in fully custom services where every project is a snowflake and nothing compounds. Or pick the delivery mechanism based on what's trendy (everyone wants a course / a membership / a digital product) rather than what your current leverage can actually support.

**Common mistake:** Confusing the product with the offer. The product is what you sell - the template, the service, the course. The offer is *how* you sell it - the dream outcome, the person, the proof. People keep adding more to the product to fix a broken offer. They build for months before validating that anyone wants it. They price by adding up their hours instead of by the result. Or they pick the wrong delivery mechanism for their stage - trying to sell a $50 digital product when they only have 100 monthly visitors (which mathematically can't hit $10K), or trying to sell a $5K done-for-you service when they have no leverage to attract that client.

**Anna's POV:** Take an **offer-first approach**. Sell people on the result first, then figure out how to deliver it. *"If you can't attract people interested in the result, your product is useless - it's helping people do something they don't want or need."* Increase value by improving four levers, not by adding more stuff: (1) **dream outcome** - a more specific, more painful problem solved with a more desirable result, (2) **perceived likelihood** of getting that result - proof, case studies, your own transformation, (3) **decrease effort and sacrifice** required, (4) **decrease time to result**.

Pick your delivery mechanism by the leverage you actually have right now - which is a function of how much traffic you can generate to a sales page each month, assuming a ~2% conversion rate:

- **~100 monthly visitors** -> done-for-you services or 1:1 coaching at **$5K+** (one-to-one)
- **~1,000 monthly visitors** -> group coaching, memberships, small cohorts, semi-custom services at **$500+** (one-to-few)
- **Several thousand+ monthly visitors** -> templates, mini-courses, communities, live workshops at **$50-$200** (one-to-many)

*"The way you get from one-to-one to one-to-many in your business is simply by increasing the traffic you can generate. Business is just a numbers game."*

The content that powers your offer should be **implementation-based, not information-based**. The best digital products solve *"a simple, annoying, urgent problem"* and deliver a quick win the buyer can use right away - not a course full of theory. *"The best digital products are notion templates, website templates, design assets, or AI/automation workflows. Avoid things that are information-based like self-guided video courses or just PDFs."*

> **Sub-framework: Product vs Offer**
> - **Product** = what you sell (content + delivery mechanism)
> - **Offer** = how you sell it (big promise + the person + proof)
> Anna's $24 Content Workflow example: the *product* is a Notion template. The *offer* is *"a proven content system to attract your first web design client in 30 days, used to go from 0 to $10K in 6 months."* Same product. The offer is what makes it valuable.

**Why it's better:** You stop building things nobody wants. You learn what sells in weeks not months. You pick the right delivery mechanism for your current leverage instead of chasing the wrong one and burning out. You stop trying to price by adding more deliverables and start pricing by results. You're free to repackage the same underlying system multiple ways as your traffic grows - a $24 template today, a $5K done-for-you service tomorrow, all built on the same body of work.

### 3. Content

> *"You are not trying to go viral. Your views, likes and followers do not matter as much as just reaching the right 25 people - the people who are actually going to buy from you."*

**Industry standard:** Post everywhere, every day. Optimize for the hook, the algorithm, the view count. Burn out on quantity. *"Just stay consistent"* for years. Treat content like a slot machine - publish and pray.

**Common mistake:** Content that gets views but doesn't convert. Hooks designed for clicks, not buyers. Audience grows but income stays flat. Trying to be on every platform at once and being mediocre on all of them. Creating disposable short-form content for a year and having nothing compounding at the end of it - the moment you stop posting, the traffic stops too. Becoming just another *"information source"* by giving generic how-to tips that get scraped by AI tomorrow.

**Anna's POV:** Long-form over short-form. **Build seven hours of trust-building content around one specific transformation.** *"A buyer will typically spend seven hours engaging with a brand's content before buying from them."* The more time someone spends with you, the more they trust you, and the more they're willing to spend with you.

A piece of long-form content (a 15-min YouTube video every other week for a year = 6.5 hours) compounds. Short-form is faster for growth, but the lifespan is short and the conversions are lower. The hybrid play: short-form to grow and test, long-form as the conversion machine, drive short-form traffic to one long-form video that does the selling for you.

The content has to do four jobs - and Anna's split:

- **20% growth** - broader content that brings new people in. You don't know what this looks like until you experiment and see what hits.
- **Bulk: nurture/value** - frameworks, processes, strategies. *Teach why, not just how. Information is a commodity; insight is rare.* This content shifts a belief. It positions you as a thought leader, not another tutorial source.
- **20% connection** - personal stories, point of view, behind the scenes, vulnerability. *"People do not buy from you because you have the best offer or the best sales page. They buy because they like and trust you as a person."*
- **20% conversion** - direct walkthroughs of your system with a direct CTA to buy.

The cornerstone move: build **one long-form video directly tied to your offer** - a video sales letter that does the converting for you. Then every other piece of content can talk freely to your dream customer without having to sell in it - just send them to that one video at the end. *"This video keeps selling for you while you're offline."*

Three rules baked into all of it:

1. **Give the knowledge away free, sell the implementation.** Gatekeeping kills trust. *"If people feel like you're giving them half the story and gatekeeping the best stuff for your paid products, they'll sense it, and it will destroy any potential trust you could have built."* Generous, full-value content + premium prices for access to you (tools, templates, coaching).
2. **Align your content with what you're already doing in your business.** Don't create extra work. If you're working with clients, share the work after each project. If you're coaching, repurpose the lessons. If you're holding Q&As, cut them up. The closer your content is to your real work, the more sustainable it is.
3. **Tell people how they can work with you.** *"The more you do this, the more conversions you'll get. Position your offer as the natural next step after watching your content - not as a hard sell."*

> **Sub-framework: The four content jobs**
> 1. Attract new people (growth, 20%)
> 2. Nurture existing audience with value (bulk)
> 3. Build connection through story and personality (20%)
> 4. Convert with a direct CTA (20%)
> In short-form, do one job per piece. In long-form, you can do all four in a single video.

**Why it's better:** Trust converts, and trust is built in hours, not seconds. One great long-form video keeps selling for years - a 24-hour sales machine that runs even when you're offline. Long-form attracts buyers; short-form attracts consumers. You don't burn out chasing daily posting because the model is depth over volume. And because every piece is tied to one transformation, the library compounds into authority instead of scattering across topics.

### 4. Launch

> *"You can't spend marbles that you don't have. You can't spend trust that you haven't built yet."*

**Industry standard:** Either the giant 5-day live launch with a complicated funnel, hype emails, fake urgency, and a launch model that takes months to build. Or the opposite: never launch, just post forever, *"stay consistent"* for one to two years, and hope something happens. Or constantly soft-launch everything with no focus, treating every week like a launch week.

**Common mistake:** Trying to launch to a cold audience that hasn't built up any trust to spend. Big launches without proof of demand, so the product flops and you have nowhere to go. Forever-consistent posting with no concentrated push, which means no real conversion moment ever happens. Burnout from trying to maintain peak intensity all the time. Or using fake urgency (countdown timers, manufactured scarcity) which *"erodes trust"* - if you say something, you have to stick to it.

**Anna's POV:** Anna uses Brene Brown's **marble jar** analogy: trust is built marble by marble through small consistent actions over time. A launch is a focused window where you **spend the trust** you've earned. Every ask spends marbles. You can't spend what you haven't built.

Launches happen in three phases:

1. **Attract** (build the audience and trust, if you don't have enough yet) - a content sprint posting more than your sustainable rate for a fixed window (e.g., 4 weeks), delivering value content, authority content, and connection content. Skip this phase only if you already have an engaged audience for the specific result you're selling.
2. **Pre-launch / validate** - directly ask if this is something people want. Post about it, build a waitlist (max 30 days, ideally shorter), use polls, reach out to your network. Use this period to gather real signal before you spend energy launching to silence.
3. **Launch** - a defined window (1-2 weeks max) where you go hard on the offer. Minimum 6 posts or 3 emails. Build in real urgency: limited spots, limited-time beta pricing, or an exclusive bonus. **No fake urgency.** Most sales happen at the start and the end - longer launches just create a sales lull in the middle.

The mechanic: **build trust, then spend it.** This is the cycle you repeat in your business - attract, validate, launch, then back to attract for the next round.

Two more Anna-specific moves baked in:

- **Launch the minimum viable version, then iterate.** Don't try to perfect the offer first. Launch the leanest possible version (one workshop, not a full cohort; one template, not a full course). Each launch cycle improves the offer based on real feedback. Raise the price each round as proof stacks. Anna's own first launch: 300 people on a waitlist, 3 emails, $1,500 in 24 hours.
- **Plan it now so you don't doubt yourself later.** *"You will doubt yourself a hundred different times in the process. So this is why we want to create a plan now so we stick to it. Then we focus on doing the actions we told ourselves we'd do and forget about the outcomes."*

> **Sub-framework: The marble jar**
> - Marbles you ADD: valuable content, frameworks, behind-the-scenes, results, personality, consistency, micro-wins for the audience.
> - Marbles you SPEND: asking the audience to buy, direct sales content, urgency reminders, follow-ups.
> - You can spend during launch what you built during attract. Empty jar = no sales, no matter how good the offer.

**Why it's better:** Faster path to first sales than slow-and-steady growth. ADHD-friendly: the intensity of a focused sprint matches how creative brains actually work, with rest periods in between. Mathematically grounded - you can only spend trust you have, which is why hyped cold launches fail. And the launch-iterate-relaunch cycle means each round gets you closer to a signature offer, instead of one giant launch that either lands or kills the project.

### 5. Scaling

> *"Your main goal right now is to develop your one core offer. Focus on one audience, on one platform, driving traffic to one offer that you're continuously iterating until you get to the point where this is really working."*

**Industry standard:** Build out everything at once. Email funnels, automations, an entire offer suite of low / mid / high ticket products, multiple lead magnets, complex CRM workflows - before you've even proven the core offer converts. *Diversify* into multiple products and audiences. Try to scale by adding more offers and more traffic sources.

**Common mistake:** Building systems before you have anything worth systematizing. Setting up elaborate email sequences for an offer that isn't selling. Building three different products for three different audiences and ending up with marketing so scattered nothing converts. Trying to scale by *acquiring more traffic* when the real lever is *making each customer worth more.* Adding a low-ticket DIY freebie when your offer is a high-ticket service, attracting the wrong audience and wasting your list. *Diversification is a distraction.*

**Anna's POV:** Scaling is the long-term systems play, but the order matters. **First**, develop one core offer that actually converts. *"This may take you an entire year. Building out systems before you can generate traffic and convert is a waste of time."* Once that works, install three systems in this order:

1. **Content ecosystem.** One platform, one audience, one offer. A library of long-form content tied to one transformation, supported by short-form for growth. This is the traffic engine.
2. **Email system.** One lead magnet that's a practical tool (not an information PDF) that matches your core offer (DIY templates attract DIY buyers; for high-ticket services use a self-assessment, not a how-to guide). One welcome sequence: 1 intro email + 3-5 nurture emails that each address a painful problem and present your offer as the solution. *"Once you have this set up, all you need to do is focus on getting people onto your email list. The emails do the converting for you."*
3. **Product suite.** Not separate products for separate audiences - the **same person, the same result, with increasing proximity and support**. Digital product (DIY) -> group coaching/community (done-with-you) -> 1:1 coaching (high-touch). Or for services: standalone strategy session -> signature service -> retainer. Each step is the *next* step for the same buyer, not a different buyer. *"The goal of your offer suite is to increase the amount each current customer spends with you - not to cater to more types of customer."*

Baked into all of this: the **front door offer** - one core offer you talk about in your content, one sales page, one main path in. *"If you give people too many options, they won't choose anything."* Get them in the door, then upsell from inside.

And the underlying belief that powers it: **the system, not willpower.** The Solopreneur OS is the human-first system that holds the whole thing together. Built around your brain, your energy, and your life. AI in service of you. *"You stop being the bottleneck in your own business."*

> **Sub-framework: Three systems, in order**
> 1. **Content ecosystem** - generates traffic
> 2. **Email system** - nurtures and converts on autopilot
> 3. **Product suite** - increases lifetime value per customer
> Don't build #2 or #3 until #1 is working. Don't build the elaborate version of any of them until the simple version is converting.

**Why it's better:** Sustainability without burnout. You stop being the bottleneck because the system carries the conversion work. The business runs while you're offline. Your traffic dollars compound because each customer is worth more, not because you've added more channels. You stay lean enough to keep iterating as AI and tools change underneath you. The end state isn't a bigger team or a bigger funnel - it's a two-hour day with a system that holds.

## How to use this file

This is the backbone of everything downstream:

- The **5 steps** become the structure of the transformation series (5 videos, one per step)
- The **per-step POVs** become the value sections inside every video
- The **sub-frameworks** become the named mechanisms Anna teaches by name (Product vs Offer, the marble jar, the 4 content jobs, the 3 ingredients of differentiation, the 3 systems in order)
- The **foundational beliefs** become the cold opens, the sales page leads, the conviction layer in every long-form piece
- The **industry standard / common mistake** framings become the contrarian hooks that pull the right person in and push the wrong person out

Every piece of content should trace back to one of the 5 steps OR one of the 2 foundational beliefs. If it doesn't, it's not on-brand for the system.
