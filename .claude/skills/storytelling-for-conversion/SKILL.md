# Storytelling for Conversion

A well-told story sells more than a list of benefits because it creates a mental movie the reader cannot ignore. The reader's heart decides; reasoning justifies afterward. This skill teaches the structures that work.

## Required vault data

- `01_Core/core_my-story.md` — the user's own origin story, stakes, turning point
- `proof.md` — customer transformation stories
- `01_Core/core_audience.md` — who the reader is, what they're carrying

See [EXAMPLES.md](EXAMPLES.md) for the creator's "Burnt out designer to six-figure solopreneur" canonical story.

## The 4-part reader-facing pattern

Use this structure for the story portion of any sales page or About page:

1. **Mirror their 'before'** — describe the situation the audience is in right now: problems, struggles, thoughts, feelings. The reader should think "that's me."
2. **Show the turning point** — the shift that needs to happen (strategy, decision, mindset). The thing the reader hasn't yet seen.
3. **Connect to their desired 'after'** — paint the desired result. Tie the story directly to the transformation the offer delivers.
4. **Shift the focus back to them** — connect the transformation to the offer with a soft CTA: "If you're ready to do the same..."

## Three story arc structures (pick one based on context)

| Arc | When to use | Shape |
|---|---|---|
| **Hero's Journey** | Long-form sales pages, About pages, founder origin stories | Pain → trigger → attempts → discovery → resolution |
| **ABT (And-But-Therefore)** | Opening hooks, social posts, short ads, intros | "I was doing X AND it worked, BUT then Y happened, THEREFORE I had to..." |
| **"They Laughed When..."** | When there's a real "everyone said this wouldn't work" moment | Skepticism → proof → vindication |

## Specificity rules (what makes stories actually convert)

Specific sensory details over abstractions. Always include:
- **Dates** ("By September 2024..." not "a few years ago")
- **Numbers** ("$1500 in the first 24 hours" not "made some money")
- **Places** ("on the phone to my mum in tears" not "I was upset")
- **Internal emotion** ("ready to give up" not "I felt down")
- **Names** (yours, the customer's, the place's)
- **Dialogue** when it lands — "She said: I'd tried everything"

Show, don't tell. "Ron looked at his bank statement: $1,847" beats "Ron had money problems."

## When to use which story type

| Story type | When |
|---|---|
| **Own story** | Founder-led offers, About pages, sales pages where the founder IS the credibility |
| **Customer story** | Case-study-led offers, services where the founder isn't the hero |
| **Relatable composite** | Early-stage offers — when no specific customer story is loud enough yet. Built from common patterns in `core_audience.md`. |

## Writing process

1. Read `core_my-story.md`, `proof.md`, `core_audience.md`
2. Pick story type (own / customer / composite) based on what the page is selling
3. Pick arc structure (Hero's Journey / ABT / "They Laughed When") based on length + context
4. Apply the 4-part reader-facing pattern as the scaffolding
5. Layer in specific details — dates, numbers, places, emotions, dialogue
6. End with the shift back to the reader: "If you're ready to do the same..."
7. Voice check — does it sound like the user, or like a stock memoir?

## Fatal mistakes

- Vague heroes the reader can't identify with ("a young entrepreneur")
- Stories without meaningful tension (no struggle = no payoff)
- Generic details ("things were tough") instead of specific ones
- Disconnection between the story's theme and the offer (cool story, but what does it have to do with what you're selling?)
- Hero stays the hero forever — never shifts the focus back to the reader
