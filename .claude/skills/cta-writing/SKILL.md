# CTA Writing

The job of a CTA is to make the right next step obvious and frictionless for the reader who is ready, while staying invisible to the reader who is not. Generic CTAs ("Get Instant Access", "Claim Your Spot") fail because they sound like every other sales page.

## Required vault data

- `01_Core/core_cta.md` — the user's CTA voice patterns and signature phrases
- `01_Core/core_voice-style.md` — voice rules, what they never say
- `01_Core/core_offer.md` — what the reader is being invited into

If any are missing, stop and name the missing file.

## The core principle: CTAs should not be pushy

A CTA should sound genuine and non-pushy. The job is to invite, not to close. If the reader feels pushed, the CTA fails — even if it converts in the short term, it costs trust.

There is no single "correct" CTA pattern. There are patterns that work because they sound like a real person, not a marketer. Below are the patterns the user uses most.

## Pattern 1: Natural insertion in content (videos, blog posts, podcasts)

The most common pattern in content. When you've just demonstrated or explained something complex that the audience would benefit from extra help with, you offer that help. Insert at natural moments:

- "If you want these templates I'm using, you can grab them down below."
- "If you want my help with this, you can grab the templates down below."
- "If you want my help with this, sign up to work with me down below."
- "If you want clarity on making this work for your business, my coaching link is down below."

These don't build or escalate across videos. They're inserted at the moment in the content where they make sense — right after the demonstration that creates the desire for more.

## Pattern 2: Soft invite + pre-dismissal (one example, not a rule)

This is one specific pattern that works because it uses curiosity + no-bullshit dismissal to sound genuine:

1. **Pre-dismiss the need.** Tell the reader they don't need this. ("You don't need me. You can absolutely do this without me.")
2. **Then offer the door.** Say where to find it if they want it. ("But if you want a community where we have cozy group calls and the templates I've built, the link is below.")

Use this when it fits the moment. Don't force every CTA into this shape.

## What to never write

- "Get Instant Access"
- "Claim Your Spot"
- "Order Now"
- "Submit"
- "Click Here"
- "Don't Miss Out"
- Any countdown urgency
- Any "limited spots" theater (unless real)
- Any "free for the next 24 hours" (unless real)

## What to write instead

- "If you want [specific thing], [link/button] is in the description."
- "And so if you're ready for that in your own business, [next step]."
- "If you want to see exactly how I [outcome], [link]."
- The button label is what they get, not the action: "Join Solopreneur Systems" not "Click Here to Buy"

## CTA placement on a long-form page

| Position | Job |
|---|---|
| **End of intro** (first CTA) | Soft, low-stakes — "If you want the full thing, scroll down or jump to the end" |
| **After the offer is introduced** | The first real ask — invite, not push |
| **After proof / examples** | Reinforcement — "If those results are what you want too, here's the link" |
| **End of page (final CTA)** | The wrap. Personal sign-off. Clean handoff. |

## CTAs in content (videos, posts) vs. on sales pages

CTAs in content are inserted at natural moments after a demonstration. They are NOT a build/arc across multiple pieces — every video can use the same kind of CTA. The pattern is: "I just showed you [complex thing]. If you want my help with it, [link]."

CTAs on sales pages DO have placement (intro, after offer, after proof, end). Same voice, different positions.

Real urgency only ever appears when it actually exists (cohort start date, price actually rising). Never invent.

## Writing process

1. Read `core_cta.md` for the user's signature phrases and patterns
2. Read `core_offer.md` for what the reader is being invited into
3. Draft the CTA using the soft-invite + pre-dismissal pattern
4. Check: would the reader feel pushed, or invited? If pushed, soften.
5. Strip any generic CTA language. Replace with what they get, in plain words.
6. For multi-CTA placement, vary phrasing — same ask, different words.

## Common failure modes

- Defaulting to button-language CTAs ("Get Started Now")
- Skipping the pre-dismissal — sounds like a hard sell
- Inventing fake urgency to push action
- Over-CTAing — every paragraph ends with a button (reader stops trusting)
- Using urgency language when there's no real urgency
