# Voice & copy

The reference work shares a copy posture across many otherwise-different brands: **short declarative taglines, confident second-person body, one strong idea per paragraph, all-caps tracked section labels.** Below is the playbook.

## The tagline

The tagline is the brand's thesis statement compressed to 2-5 words. Always.

Patterns from the reference work:

| Brand archetype | Tagline pattern | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Wellness, return-to-self | Verb-led, second-person, gentle | "Flow Back To You" · "Begin Again" · "Soft Return" |
| Performance / sport | Imperative, slightly defiant | "Play with Purpose." · "All In On You" · "Don't Sweat It." |
| Beauty / category-disrupt | Question or contrarian statement | "Why isn't there makeup made for women who sweat?" · "Made for the Real Skin Day" |
| Beverage / cultural | Possession, sensory | "The new social seltzer" · "Say Goodbye to Hangovers" · "Brought to you by 4pm." |
| Editorial coach / founder | Verb-stack, aspirational | "Lead with purpose." · "Form with feeling." · "Done with shrinking." |
| Personal brand / portfolio | Single noun phrase or fragment | "Real Users, Life Changing Results" · "What you already own." |

Rules:
- Active voice
- Second person preferred
- No corporate jargon ("solutions," "leverage," "optimize")
- No hype ("the only," "revolutionary," "game-changing")
- One full stop is fine. Avoid exclamation marks. They cheapen the line.
- The tagline should make sense when paired with the wordmark and a single photograph. If it needs three lines of subtext to land, it is not the tagline yet.

## Body copy structure

**One strong idea per paragraph.** Paragraphs are short. 2-4 sentences typical. The reading rhythm is: confident statement → context or proof → next confident statement.

**Narrow measure.** Body copy is set in 45-65 character columns. This is the editorial reading width that magazines have used for a century. Wider columns (full-width 80ch+) read like blog posts; narrower (under 40ch) read like Instagram captions. 56ch is the sweet spot.

**Bolded sentences inside paragraphs.** A single sentence per paragraph can be bolded as the takeaway. Used sparingly - one bolded sentence per section, not per paragraph.

**Quoted phrasing.** Short quoted phrases inside body copy ("a word that feels both grounded and fluid") are the editorial signature. Use when the brand language deserves emphasis without becoming a separate pull quote.

**Second person dominant.** "You" is the subject. The brand speaks to the reader, not about itself. "We" appears when accountability is being claimed; otherwise minimize.

### Sample body, on-taste

> Mira is grown on a single hillside in Umbria, picked by hand within forty-eight hours of pressing, and bottled within the week. **Nothing about it is fast.** That is the point.
>
> You do not need another oil. You need the one you actually reach for - the one that earns its place on the counter, not the one stashed in the cabinet for guests.

### Sample body, drifting toward generic

> Mira is a premium small-batch olive oil crafted with care from the finest Italian olives. Our innovative process ensures every bottle delivers exceptional quality and a truly unique flavor experience that stands apart from the competition.

The first sample names places, times, and a refusal. The second is a string of category-default adjectives. The first is on-taste. The second could be any olive oil brand on Earth.

## Section labels (eyebrows)

ALL CAPS. Tracked +0.10em to +0.16em. Set in the body sans, not the display serif. Often paired with a thin horizontal rule above or below.

The reference work uses a small standard set:

```
OVERVIEW
SERVICES
PROCESS
APPROACH
RESULTS
PRESS
CONTACT
JOURNAL
```

For brand sites (not portfolios), the labels follow the brand language but stay in this register:

```
PROVENANCE
THE GROVE
OUR PROCESS
HOW IT'S MADE
THE BOTTLE
WHERE TO FIND IT
```

Avoid:
- Sentence-case section headings ("Our process") - too soft, reads as a generic blog
- Headings without eyebrows when the section is brand-defining - the eyebrow is what gives the editorial register
- Eyebrow + heading + subheading + body. That is too many levels. Eyebrow + heading + body is plenty.

## CTA copy

The reference brands use direct CTA phrasing. Verb-first, low-pressure, specific. Never "Click here," "Learn more," or "Get started" alone - those are placeholder copy.

Patterns that work:

```
Read the journal
See the work
Find a bottle
Visit the grove
Book a consult
Start with one
```

For the creator's voice rule (and as a general principle from her cta-writing skill): CTAs should not be pushy. Soft-invite + pre-dismissal beats hard-sell. "If this is your kind of thing, here's where to start" beats "Get yours now before it's gone."

## What to never do in copy

- Em dashes (the rule across this entire vault). Use hyphens or colons or full stops.
- Lorem ipsum + emoji bullets. Either real copy or a colored block placeholder, never both.
- "We" repeated as the subject of every paragraph. The reader does not care about you; they care about themselves seen through you.
- Adjective stacks ("premium, exceptional, innovative, cutting-edge"). One adjective is plenty. Often zero is better.
- Generic benefit phrasing ("save time, reduce stress, achieve more"). Specific stories beat abstract benefits.
- Hashtag-style fragments ("#authentic #real #unfiltered"). Brand voice does not need self-tagging.
- Fake scarcity / urgency ("only 5 left," "ends midnight"). Editorial brands trust the reader.
- Industry jargon. If you would not say it across a dinner table, do not write it on the page.

## Voice calibration questions

Before writing copy for any brand, answer:

1. What does this brand sound like when it is **confident** but not arrogant? (Most brands fail by overshooting confidence into hype.)
2. What does this brand sound like when it is **warm** but not cute? (Most brands fail by overshooting warm into "we love our customers!" energy.)
3. What does this brand sound like when it is **specific** but not over-explainy? (Most brands fail by stacking three sentences of context where one would do.)
4. What is the one sentence the founder would say to a friend at dinner about why this exists?

That last one is usually the tagline, hiding in plain sight.
